Abstract and Figures

The inherent limits of traditional diagnoses and therapies have driven the development and application of emerging nanotechnologies for more effective and safer management of diseases, herein referred to as 'nanotheranostics'. Although many important technological successes have been achieved in this field, widespread adoption of nanotheranostics as a new paradigm is hindered by specific obstacles, including time-consuming synthesis of nanoparticles, incomplete understanding of nano-bio interactions, and challenges regarding chemistry, manufacturing and the controls required for clinical translation and commercialization. As a key branch of artificial intelligence, machine learning (ML) provides a set of tools capable of performing time-consuming and result-perception tasks, thus offering unique opportunities for nanotheranostics. This Review summarizes the progress and challenges in this emerging field of ML-aided nanotheranostics, and discusses the opportunities in developing next-generation nanotheranostics with reliable datasets and advanced ML models to offer better clinical benefits to patients.

Organic nanoparticles
Organic nanoparticles applied in theranostics mainly include
lipid-based nanoparticles (for example, liposomes and LNPs)55 and poly-
meric (for example, poly(lactic acid) (PLA) and poly(lactic-co-glycolic
acid) (PLGA)) nanoparticles2. These organic nanoparticles have distinct
structures and functions that provide specific physical, chemical and
biological properties, enabling their applications in drug delivery, tis-
sue engineering and immunotherapy42,56. The synthesis of organic nano-
particles involves numerous parameters, including temperature, pH,
and the kinds and concentrations of monomers, initiators, crosslinkers
and catalysts, which collectively influence the size, hydrophobicity and
surface activity of nanoparticles. These properties play a crucial role
in determining the performance of nanoparticles in their theranostic
applications, such as drug delivery and tissue engineering

NanoРђЊbiomolecule interactions
The introduction of nanoparticles into biological fluids results in the
formation of a biomolecular corona
75
, where proteins, lipids, sugar
moieties, nucleic acids and metabolites spontaneously adsorb onto
the nanoparticle surface, creating a surrogate biological identity
60
.
While previous studies have primarily focused on the adsorption of
proteins onto nanoparticle surface
60,74
, this part specifically concen-
trates on the nanoРђЊbiomolecule interactions related to protein corona.
Although recent advancements have made great progress towards
high-throughput approaches, experimental protocols characterizing
the protein corona on various synthesized nanoparticles in various
biological environments are laborious and costly
74
. Current strategies
characterizing the corona rely on mass spectrometry-based proteom-
ics, which is expensive and laborious, and requires high-level technical
experts
75
. ML models trained with comprehensive multiomics-based
datasets generated by mass spectrometry methods offer a solution
with great potential to overcome these technical obstacles, enabling
efficient and reliable prediction of protein adsorption onto nanopar-
ticles and their associated biological impacts32,78 (Fig. 4a).
RF classification, which combines multiple decision trees to create
a predictive model, shows great promise in training with mass spec-
trometry datasets to identify the corona features79,80. For instance, an
RF algorithm achieved 78% accuracy and 70% precision in predicting
the proteins adsorbed onto single-walled carbon nanotubes by using 38
protein features derived from amino acid sequences81. Moreover, kernel
density estimates (statistical methods used to estimate variable distri-
butions) were applied to reveal a notable correlation between the pro-
tein characteristics (that is, enhanced solvent-exposed glycine residues
and high leucine residues) and the prediction of proteins that either
adsorb or desorb from the single-walled carbon nanotubes. Given the
diversity and complexity of proteins, current RF classifiers developed
for corona prediction primarily rely on protein-based features (for
example, pH, isoelectric point, amino acid compositions, molecular
weight and hydrodynamic size)80,81. However, limited characterizable
properties of nanoparticles hinder their inclusion as effective features
during the ML modelling82. Consequently, incorporating more diverse
and a greater quantity of solvent and nanoparticle features is crucial
to improve the corona prediction.

ML-enhanced nanotheranostic applications
An ideal clinical treatment usually requires precisely delivering the right
type of therapy to the right place, thus achieving localized manage-
ment of disease with minimal toxicity

. This is a daunting task because
it necessitates careful coordination of clinical detection, molecular
imaging and precise therapy2,4,98. Nanotechnologies have emerged as
a promising solution for this campaign, leading to the development
of a wide range of nanotheranostic platforms with diagnostic and
therapeutic capabilities for enhanced disease management1,7. Recent
significant improvement in computing power and big data have greatly
facilitated the development of ML

for nanotheranostic applications, especially in improving the accuracy
of clinical detection and molecular imaging, and the efficacy of treat-
ment strategies and/or drugs20,25,49,100.

Nano-mediated therapy
Considering that the effectiveness of nanoparticle therapeutics
relies on two key elements, delivery nanocarriers and encapsulated
drugs4,65,67, this section emphasizes the utilization of ML in development
and refinement of delivery nanocarriers and encapsulated drugs, with
a specific focus on nucleic acids and peptides (Fig. 5c).
Developing targeted delivery carriers involves complicated
synthetic routes that encompass self-assembly and chemical
modification16,35. On the basis of the molecular structures of drugs them-
selves, a relevant study developed quantitative structureРђЊnanoparticle
assembly prediction (QSNAP) models to precisely predict self-assembly
of nanoparticles40. The prepared nanoparticles guided by QSNAP
showed selective targeting of kinase inhibitors to caveolin-1-expressing
human colorectal and hepatic carcinoma models, resulting in nota-
ble therapeutic effects while minimizing adverse effects in healthy
tissues40. In a follow-up study based on a high-throughput experi-
ment platform, the ML model predicted 100 self-assembling drug
nanoparticles out of 2.1РђЅmillion pairs, each comprising one of 2,686
approved excipients and one of 788 drug candidates130. After optimiza-
tion, this ML model showed an impressive performance in predicting
1.8% of all possible pairs that can co-aggregate into nanoparticles

Self-assembling nanoparticles have been widely used for sustained
drug delivery, which is a highly promising strategy for treating chronic
diseases due to its potential to enhance therapeutic safety, efficacy
and patient compliance

multifunctional peptides that efficiently enter cells, bind to melanin
and have low cytotoxicity

combined with a drug used to lower intraocular pressure (that is, bri-
monidine), sustained reduction in intraocular pressure was observed
in rabbits for up to 18РђЅdays after a single intracameral injection.
Despite their widespread use in the fight against COVID-19, mRNA
vaccines still have notable drawbacks attributed to mRNA instability57,59.

structures and codons, and the design space of mRNA is vast and chal-

SARS-CoV-2 spike protein, enabling improved mRNA half-life, increased
protein expression and a remarkable up to 128-fold rise in antibody
titre compared with the codon-optimization benchmark
133

nucleic acids (SNAs) are nanoparticles that are functionalized with a
shell of DNA or RNA and show robust immune activation compared with
free antigens and oligonucleotides134. A recent study used kNN-based

QSAR model, enabling optimization of SNA design and prediction of
immune activation by SNAs

immunotherapy and anti-inflammatory treatment. Antimicrobial pep-

clinical applications are limited by poor stability, low penetration and

modules for classification, selection, ranking and regression tasks was
created to systematically identify potent AMPs from a highly extensive
library of candidates consisting of six to nine amino acids135. In mouse
models with bacterial pneumonia, the leading three AMPs identified
by the pipeline showed comparable therapeutic efficacy to penicillin,
negligible toxicity and low possibility to induce drug resistance135.
Challenges and opportunities
This Review highlights the unique opportunities in ML-aided nanothera-
nostics, but it also emphasizes the equally significant challenges that
need to be addressed in this field. Although promising, most previous
studies in ML-aided nanotheranostics have predominantly relied on
conventional and interpretable ML models such as linear aggregation,
SVM and RF
36,37,58,114,130
. However, there has been a recent surge in the use
of more advanced deep learning (DL) techniques, which have shown
remarkable success across various intricate tasks, including image
and video generation, as evidenced by the success of ChatGPT and the
recent Sora
136,137
. These advanced DL methods, often employing CNNs
or transformers, show enhanced capability in addressing complex chal-
lenges but typically demand larger volumes of reliable data17,31. Thus,
the most central and critical challenge in ML-aided nanotheranostics
is the urgent requirement for large, reliable and well-annotated data
for training ML models
18,138
. This section discusses data-related chal-
lenges, including data size, diversity and interpretability challenges
in this field, and proposes potential solutions as well.
Data size challenges
It is the age of big data right now, where extensive data can be collected
across multiple layers of health monitoring and clinical theranostics20.
Despite the extraordinary complexity, the majority of these data are
still an order of magnitude too small to provide practical training for ML
modelling
19
. Interestingly, an ML method has been applied to recognize
the clinical trials involving nanodrugs and nanodevices from Clinical-
Trials.gov139. The results show that although ML has been used for drug
discovery for decades, there are far fewer examples and datasets avail-
able for ML model building in the field of nanotheranostics
138
. There
are several options for this challenge, with the first and most immedi-
ate being the investment in the collection of meticulously curated
and highly reliable РђўnanoinformaticРђЎ datasets specifically designed for
cutting-edge studies in nanotheranostics
140
, while another alternative
is to generate in silico data that mimic the properties of real data26. For
example, data augmentation, a frequently used approach for image
analysis, can be employed to relieve the scarcity of training data issues
18
.
Data diversity challenges
Another challenge lies in integrating diverse multimodal and multi-
scale nanotheranostic data140. Unlike more conventional data types
such as images, videos or texts, which lend themselves to straightfor-
ward feature extraction, nanotheranostic data present complexities
that hinder effective feature engineering116, resulting in difficulties in
optimization and generalization that can adversely affect the real-life
prediction performance of the model
122
. For example, in most stud-
ies on ML-aided nanotheranostics, only a limited number of possible
physicochemical properties of nanoparticles are considered; therefore,
nanoparticles are not described completely, which limits the modelling
performance, reduces the prediction reliability and finally decreases
the efficiency of nanotheranostic applications
32,65
. Several strategies
can be used to overcome this challenge, such as collecting data with
maximum performance, using large and diverse sample sizes, apply-
ing domain knowledge to guide feature selection, analysing principal
components, and employing appropriate training, rigorous validation
and comprehensive monitoring40,141.

All in all, there is a long way to go to decode and leverage the
complexities of nanotechnologies and theranostic platforms, and,
